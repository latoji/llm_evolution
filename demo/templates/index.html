Text Predictor

Type a few words and this app will try to continue your sentence — like a smart autocomplete

This app learned common word patterns from classic books and other texts. When you type a prompt, it looks at the last few words and guesses what comes next — one word at a time, just like the autocomplete on your phone.

How it works

1
Break your text into pieces BPE Tokenisation

Your sentence is chopped into small pieces called tokens using Byte Pair Encoding (BPE) — the same method used by GPT-2. Think of it like breaking a sentence into Scrabble tiles. The app knows about ~1200 different pieces.

2
Look at the last few words N-gram context window

The app reads the last 4 pieces (the n-gram context) and checks: “In the training texts, what usually came after these pieces?” It counts how often each possible next piece appeared in that position.

3
Handle unseen patterns Kneser-Ney back-off smoothing

If the app has never seen your exact combination, it doesn’t give up. Using Modified Kneser-Ney smoothing, it backs off to shorter patterns (3 words, then 2, then 1) until it finds something it recognises. Every word gets at least a small chance.

4
Pick the next word Temperature + Top-k + Nucleus sampling

The app scores all candidate words, then applies your Creativity (temperature scaling), Word choices (top-k), and Focus (nucleus / top-p) sliders to filter the list. One word is randomly drawn, added to the text, and the process repeats.

Good to know:  This app only looks at the last few words (the context window), so it can’t keep track of a topic across a long paragraph. It doesn’t actually understand language — it just knows which words tend to appear next to each other. Think of it as a much fancier version of your phone’s autocomplete. These are exactly the limitations that modern neural networks (transformers) were invented to solve.
